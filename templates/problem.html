Problem Statement

In real-world insurance systems, not all claims are created equal. While the majority of claims result in relatively small payouts, a small fraction of extreme losses accounts for a disproportionate share of total cost. These rare but severe events are what ultimately drive profitability, solvency, and long-term risk exposure.

During my exploration of insurance loss data, it became clear that traditional modeling approaches‚Äîfocused primarily on average outcomes‚Äî are poorly suited for this setting. What truly matters in practice is not the ‚Äútypical‚Äù claim, but the worst plausible losses that occur in the tail of the distribution.

Why Claim Severity Modeling Matters

Claim severity estimation plays a critical role in pricing policies, setting loss reserves, allocating capital, and meeting regulatory solvency requirements. Even small systematic underestimation of severe claims can lead to material financial stress for insurers.

From a business perspective, the cost of underpredicting a high-loss claim is far greater than overpredicting a small one. This inherent asymmetry makes the problem fundamentally different from standard regression tasks encountered in many machine learning applications.

Dataset Overview

This project is based on the Allstate Claims Severity dataset, originally released as a Kaggle competition. Each observation represents an individual insurance claim, described by a combination of continuous and high-cardinality categorical features.

Although the features are anonymized, the dataset closely mirrors the complexity of real insurance data: high dimensionality, heterogeneous feature types, and a highly skewed target variable.

Core Challenges

  • Extremely skewed loss distribution with a heavy right tail
  • Rare but high-impact claims that dominate total loss exposure
  • High-dimensional feature space driven largely by categorical inputs
  • Limited interpretability due to anonymized feature definitions

Why Quantile Regression?

To directly address these challenges, I adopted a quantile regression framework. Rather than predicting an average claim cost, the model estimates conditional upper-tail quantiles such as the 90th percentile.

This approach aligns naturally with insurance decision-making. Quantile estimates provide explicit, risk-aware thresholds that can be used for stress testing, conservative pricing, and capital adequacy planning.

Project Objective

The objective of this project is to build a robust, well-calibrated quantile regression model that produces reliable upper-tail loss estimates on unseen claims ‚Äî and to present these predictions through an interactive system that emphasizes interpretability and risk awareness.
